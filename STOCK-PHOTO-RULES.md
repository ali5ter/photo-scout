# Stock Photo Rules: People, Property, and Places

A practical reference for what requires a release, what to avoid, and what is safe to submit.

## People

### The core rule

If a person can identify themselves in the photo, you need their **signed model release** before
the image can be sold for commercial use. This applies regardless of where the photo was taken —
including public streets and public events.

### What requires a model release

- Any identifiable individual (face visible, recognisable features)
- Children in any identifiable form — the release must be signed by a parent or legal guardian
- Groups where individuals are distinguishable, even if small in frame

### What does not require a model release

- Crowds where no single person is individually identifiable
- Silhouettes with no identifying features
- People photographed from behind (no face, no distinguishing marks)
- Isolated body parts: hands, feet, arms — where no identity can be inferred
- People small enough in frame that identity cannot be determined

### Editorial exception

Photos of identifiable people can be sold without a model release under an **editorial licence**,
which covers news, documentary, and illustrative use. However:

- Editorial images cannot be used in advertising or commercial promotion
- They must be accurately captioned (who, what, where, when)
- They command lower royalties and have a narrower buyer market
- Shutterstock, Adobe Stock, and Alamy all have a separate editorial track

## Property and Places

### The core property rule

A **property release** is required for commercial use of any private property where the owner
could object to the image being used to sell or promote something. Public spaces and generic
exteriors are generally safe; recognisable private interiors and restricted landmarks are not.

### Public spaces and landscapes

| Subject | Safe to submit? |
| --- | --- |
| Public streets, parks, squares | Yes |
| Generic countryside, coastline, mountains | Yes |
| Public transport infrastructure (roads, bridges, stations) | Usually yes |
| National parks and public land | Yes |
| Public gardens | Usually yes — check for privately managed sections |

### Architecture

| Subject | Safe to submit? |
| --- | --- |
| Historic buildings (pre-20th century) | Usually yes — architecture copyright expires |
| Generic modern buildings (anonymous office blocks, etc.) | Usually yes |
| Architecturally distinctive modern buildings | Check — may be under copyright if architect died < 70 years ago |
| The Eiffel Tower (daytime) | Yes — the structure is public domain |
| The Eiffel Tower (night light show) | No — the light display is a copyrighted artistic work |
| The Shard, Gherkin, Lloyds Building, Centre Pompidou | Caution — architectural copyright may apply |
| Interior of any building | Property release required for commercial use |
| Private property exterior, shot from public land | Generally photographable; commercial use may still need a release |

### Art, sculpture, and cultural works

| Subject | Safe to submit? |
| --- | --- |
| Works where the artist died 70+ years ago | Yes (public domain in most jurisdictions) |
| Works where the artist is alive or died < 70 years ago | No — copyright still applies |
| Public murals and street art | Caution — the artist retains copyright unless the work is explicitly public domain |
| Museum exhibits | Property release from the museum usually required |
| Currency, stamps, official seals | Restricted — most jurisdictions prohibit commercial reproduction |

### Logos, brands, and trademarks

**Never submit an image where a brand logo, trademark, or product name is clearly legible.**
This includes:

- Car badges and manufacturer logos
- Brand names on clothing (even partially visible)
- Food and drink packaging
- Tech product branding (Apple logo, etc.)
- Shop signage prominently in frame

The image will be rejected by reviewers, and submitting it exposes the stock site (and potentially
you) to trademark infringement claims. Shooting around logos — using angle, depth of field, or
framing to obscure them — is the standard approach.

## Practical Guidelines by Subject

### Lowest risk (fewest restrictions)

- Nature, wildlife, landscapes with no built environment
- Abstract and macro photography
- Food and drink (no branded packaging)
- Generic architecture from before the 20th century
- Technology concepts (hands on keyboards, anonymous devices)
- Hands, feet, and anonymous body parts

### Medium risk (check before submitting)

- Street photography with crowds (identifiability threshold)
- Modern landmark buildings (check architectural copyright)
- Contemporary public art and sculpture
- Vehicles (obscure or crop out manufacturer badges)
- Interiors of any kind (property release)

### High risk (avoid without releases)

- Identifiable individuals without a signed model release
- Children without parental consent
- Recognisable private property interiors
- Any image with legible brand logos or trademarks
- Night photography of the Eiffel Tower light display
- Works by living artists or artists who died within 70 years

## Editorial vs. Commercial Licences

Most stock platforms offer both licence types. Understanding the distinction helps you decide
whether a borderline image is worth submitting.

| | Commercial licence | Editorial licence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Advertising and promotion | Yes | No |
| Books, magazines, editorial articles | Yes | Yes |
| News and documentary | Yes | Yes |
| Model/property release required | Yes (identifiable people/property) | No |
| Royalty rate | Higher | Lower |
| Buyer market | Broad | Narrower |

When in doubt about releases, submitting as **editorial only** keeps the image in the catalogue
and earning — Alamy in particular has a strong editorial market and accepts content that
Shutterstock and Adobe Stock would reject outright for commercial use.

---

## Platform-Specific Notes

- **Adobe Stock** — strict on model/property releases; clear editorial track; good royalties
- **Shutterstock** — high volume, tight review; requires explicit editorial flag for release-free
  people shots
- **Alamy** — most permissive with editorial content; accepts a wider range of niche and
  documentary images; higher royalty percentage
- **Pond5** — primarily video but accepts photos; useful for travel and event content
